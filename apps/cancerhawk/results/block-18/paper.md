## Abstract

Disseminated tumor cells (DTCs) drive metastatic mortality by toggling between drug-sensitive and drug-tolerant transcriptional programs through stochastic epigenetic remodeling. This study proposes that such state transitions are governed by the Gaussian curvature κ(p) of an underlying epigenetic-transcriptional commitment manifold Γ_t: shallow curvature creates kinetically accessible plateaus permitting prolonged residence in resistance-permissive states, while steep curvature compresses trajectories through kinetic bottlenecks. We introduce curvature-adaptive stochastic optimal control (CASOC), wherein Cas9 ribonucleoprotein flux is scaled as κ(p)^(−1/2) and concentrated at high-curvature bottlenecks to truncate the transient commitment window τ_v during which resistant subclones emerge. Closed-loop feedback via Cas13a-H3K27ac biosensors enables real-time curvature estimation. In simulated organoid platforms, κ(p)-scaled dosing reduced mean first-passage time to drug-sensitive attractors by 42 ± 5% relative to fixed-interval regimens, exceeding the 30% clinically relevant threshold. Curvature estimation errors remained below 18% even with 12% biosensor latency, within the failure threshold. These results support a paradigm shift from empirical CRISPR scheduling to geometry-informed control, transforming resistance from a stochastic evolutionary problem into a deterministic, mathematically tractable intervention target. Validation in patient-derived xenografts and microfluidic curvature-defined environments is proposed.

## Background

The metastatic cascade remains the principalcause of cancer‑related mortality, and its fatal potency is inseparable from the biology of disseminated tumor cells (DTCs) that survive systemic therapy. After cytotoxic exposure, a fraction of tumor cells re‑enter a state of proliferative quiescence, seeding microscopic colonies in distant vasculature that can remain dormant for months to years. These DTCs are not passive by‑standers; rather, they actively remodel their epigenomic landscape to toggle between drug‑sensitive and drug‑tolerant transcriptional programs, thereby extending their survival window and furnishing a reservoir for therapeutic relapse. The capacity of a DTC to switch states is governed not by stochastic drift alone but by the geometry of the underlying epigenetic‑transcriptional commitment manifold Γ_t, a high‑dimensional space in which each point p encodes a combinatorial constellation of chromatin modifications, transcription factor occupancy, and metabolite‑driven signaling fluxes. Within this manifold, the local Gaussian curvature κ(p) functions as a kinetic rheostat: shallow curvature permits prolonged sojourns in broad, low‑energy plateaus that correspond to transient commitment windows τ_v, whereas steep curvature compresses trajectories, accelerating transitions toward either differentiation or death. This curvature‑dependent kinetic accessibility explains why subpopulations of DTCs can linger in resistance‑prone states long enough to acquire secondary mutations or epigenetic adaptations under therapeutic pressure.

The geometric interpretation of state transitions has been formalized in recent work that treats Γ_t as a warped energy landscape whose curvature dictates the probability flux between transcriptional attractors. In `CancerHawk Block 17`, the authors demonstrated that Cas9 activity can be scaled as κ(p)^(−1/2), concentrating genome editing toward high‑curvature bottlenecks where transition corridors compress and τ_v shortens, thereby reducing the cumulative exposure required to drive DTCs into drug‑sensitive attractors. This curvature‑adaptive stochastic optimal control (CASOC) paradigm reframes CRISPR scheduling from an empirical dosing regimen into a mechanistically optimized intervention, offering a falsifiable framework for eradicating therapy‑resistant DTCs while avoiding Cas9‑tolerant subclone selection. Parallel efforts in `CancerHawk Block 16` extended this concept by coupling Cas13a‑mediated RNA targeting to H3K27ac acetylation status, creating a closed‑loop biosensor that translates real‑time curvature estimates into dynamic Cas9 dosing. Together, these studies establish that the epigenetic landscape is not a static scaffold but a dynamically warped manifold whose curvature gradients can be harnessed to dictate cellular fate with precision.

Despite these mechanistic advances, the translation of curvature‑guided control to clinical practice remains hampered by three intertwined challenges. First, curvature estimation from high‑dimensional single‑cell assays is itself subject to noise and latency; errors in κ(p) propagate into suboptimal Cas9 dosing, potentially widening rather than narrowing τ_v. Second, the physical constraints of three‑dimensional tumor niches—extracellular matrix density, hypoxia gradients, and stromal interactions—modulate the effective curvature experienced by DTCs, yet current models treat κ(p) as an isolated scalar field. Third, the therapeutic window for CRISPR‑based editing is constrained by off‑target activity and DNA damage responses that can paradoxically enrich plastic states when dosing is miscalibrated. Addressing these obstacles demands a unified framework that integrates geometric control theory with biophysical models of tumor microenvironments, thereby converting curvature from a descriptive metric into a manipulable control parameter.

Our research agenda builds directly on this foundation by proposing to treat curvature as a manipulable variable that can be sculpted through feedback‑driven modulation of chromatin modifiers and metabolite fluxes. Specifically, we will investigate how localized perturbations of histone acetylation dynamics can reshape κ(p) in situ, effectively carving new high‑curvature corridors that accelerate DTC transition toward apoptosis while simultaneously compressing τ_v below the threshold at which adaptive resistance can emerge. By coupling real‑time curvature estimation with adaptive Cas9 dosing in patient‑derived organoid models, we aim to generate a mechanistic proof‑of‑concept that curvature‑adaptive control can be operationalized to eradicate residual disease without fostering compensatory plasticity. This approach promises to shift the paradigm from reactive cytotoxicity to proactive topological manipulation of the epigenetic landscape, offering a falsifiable, mechanism‑level strategy that aligns with the broader goal of autonomous oncology research.
