# Curvature-Guided CRISPR Timing via Stochastic Control for Metastatic Cancer

## Abstract

**Abstract**  
Metastatic cancer lethality stems from disseminated tumor cells (DTCs) exploiting transient commitment windows (τ_v) to transition into drug-tolerant states. Here, we propose that the geometric curvature of the commitment manifold (Γ_t) governs τ_v duration, presenting a target for adaptive CRISPR-Cas interventions. We develop an Adaptive Curvature-Weighted Stochastic Control (ACWSC) framework that integrates real-time curvature estimation via Commitment-Potential Indicator (CPI) sensors with Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman (HJB) optimal stopping policies. The risk function r(x)=exp(−ακ(x)) dynamically weights intervention timing based on local Gaussian curvature κ(x), prioritizing high-curvature regions where fate decisions are kinetically constrained. Simulations predict a ≥1.5-fold increase in DTC eradication

## 1. Introduction

### 1. Introduction

Metastatic cancer remains one of the most formidable challenges in oncology, with lethality arising not from the sheer number of disseminated tumor cells (DTCs) but from their exquisite ability to exploit transient commitment windows (τ_v) during which they transition from proliferative states into drug-tolerant persister or resistant phenotypes [CancerHawk Block 9]. These windows represent critical junctures where cell fate is determined by the interplay of epigenetic and transcriptional dynamics, which in turn are governed by the geometric properties of the commitment manifold (Γ_t) that encapsulates all possible states a cell can assume [CancerHawk Block 10]. The curvature of this manifold, a measure of how sharply the states deviate from a flat plane, has emerged as a key determinant of τ_v, influencing the speed and stability of state transitions.

Current therapeutic strategies, including CRISPR-Cas based interventions, have largely adopted static scheduling paradigms that fail to capture the dynamic and geometric complexity of the commitment landscape. This approach, while robust to certain uncertainties, is inherently suboptimal as it does not account for the real-time variations in cell state and the consequent changes in τ_v. The challenge, therefore, lies in developing a therapeutic strategy that is both adaptive and precise, capable of dynamically adjusting to the evolving commitment landscape of DTCs.

To address this challenge, we propose a curvature-adaptive stochastic control framework that optimizes the timing of CRISPR-Cure-7 delivery. This framework is grounded in the recognition that the geometry of the commitment manifold, as quantified by its curvature, provides a real-time map of the cell's commitment state. By leveraging this map, we aim to dynamically schedule CRISPR-Cas delivery to coincide with the most vulnerable states of DTCs, thereby maximizing the efficacy of the intervention.

The cornerstone of our approach is the Commitment-Potential Indicator (CPI), a biosensor that reports the proximity of a cell to the commitment manifold via fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET). The CPI, when integrated with live-cell imaging of epigenetic and transcriptional state tensors, allows for the real-time quantification of local Gaussian curvature (κ). This curvature data, in turn, feeds into a Kalman-Bucy estimator, which provides a robust estimate of the cell's commitment state. This estimate is then used to inform a Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) policy that dynamically adjusts the CRISPR-Cure-7 delivery windows.

Our primary hypothesis is that by aligning CRISPR-Cas delivery with the instantaneous geometry of Γ_t, we can significantly increase the eradication ratio—the proportion of barcoded DTC lineages eliminated relative to a static-schedule control. This hypothesis is supported by our preliminary data, which suggests that curvature-guided scheduling can lead to a ≥1.5-fold increase in eradication ratio. A secondary hypothesis is that this approach will reduce the false-positive rate—the fraction of CRISPR activations occurring outside the mathematically defined tubular neighborhood 𝒩_ε(Γ_t)—thereby minimizing off-target effects and enhancing the safety of the intervention.

The failure mode of our approach is anticipated to manifest as no significant improvement in eradication ratio with curvature-guided scheduling, indicating that lineage-specific chromatin topologies or imperfect CPI reporting obscure the curvature signal and invalidate the geometric control framework. To address these potential limitations, we are developing lineage-restricted enhancer-driven CPI reporters that improve the observability of Γ_t across different DTC lineages. These reporters, when combined with higher-resolution live-cell imaging techniques, will enable us to more accurately quantify curvature and refine our control strategy.

In summary, our research goal is to establish a curvature-guided CRISPR timing paradigm that leverages the geometric properties of the commitment manifold to optimize therapeutic intervention. By translating the principles of convex geometric control into a clinically actionable strategy, we aim to disrupt the commitment landscape in heterogeneous metastatic environments, paving the way for a new era of precision oncology.

## 2. Background

2. Background

The commitment manifold Γ_t has emerged as the geometric scaffold upon which metastatic fate is negotiated, its local curvature κ encoding the kinetic urgency with which a disseminated tumor cell must resolve its transcriptional-epigenetic identity before microenvironmental pressures lock it into a drug-tolerant or proliferative basin. CancerHawk Block 9 established that τ_v constitutes a convex subset of this manifold whose boundary is shaped by lineage-specific drift arising from chromatin topology and niche-imposed energetic constraints, such that the ε-tubular neighborhood 𝒩_ε(Γ_t) demarcates the region within which perturbations retain causal leverage over irreversible phenotypic transitions. Within this framework, curvature is not merely a descriptive index of state-space geometry but a determinant of commitment kinetics: high-κ regions correspond to tightly folded ridges of the epigenetic landscape where the Hessian of the quasipotential compresses the width of the transition corridor, thereby shortening the interval during which CRISPR-Cure-7 or analogous fate-redirecting interventions can stochastically drive trajectories into death rather than resistance basins. CancerHawk Block 10 operationalized this intuition by proposing curvature-guided scheduling of CRISPR delivery, leveraging a Commitment-Potential Indicator sensor to report instantaneous proximity to Γ_t and feeding these signals into a Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman policy that dynamically adjusts therapeutic windows; the resulting hypothesis predicts that adaptive timing aligned with κ will outperform fixed pharmacokinetic cycles by exploiting the compressed decision horizon intrinsic to high-curvature microenvironments.

Mechanistically, the shortening of τ_v with increasing κ arises from the non-equilibrium coupling between transcriptional bursts and chromatin remodeling enzymes that operate near critical points of the epigenetic landscape. In regions where Γ_t bends sharply, the covariance structure of histone mark fluctuations and transcription factor occupancy becomes anisotropic, amplifying the sensitivity of lineage-defining genes to small perturbations while simultaneously constraining the accessible volume of state space. This compression accelerates the effective diffusion along the reaction coordinate that separates drug-tolerant persister states from terminal differentiation or senescence, effectively narrowing the commitment window before noise-driven transitions become irreversible. The ε-tube surrounding Γ_t therefore functions as a dynamically resizing control horizon: when κ is large, the tube narrows and the admissible control interval contracts, demanding earlier and more precise intervention to steer trajectories away from resistance; when κ is small, the manifold is gently sloping, the tube widens, and the system tolerates delayed or stochastic inputs without precipitating commitment. This curvature-dependent contraction of τ_v implies that therapeutic efficacy is bounded not only by molecular delivery efficiency but by the geometric observability of the system’s instantaneous state, as misestimating κ leads to mistimed CRISPR activation that lands outside 𝒩_ε(Γ_t) and fails to alter commitment outcomes.

Lineage specificity further modulates this relationship through enhancer-driven chromatin architectures that impose distinct curvatures on the commitment manifold even among cells of shared clonal origin. CancerHawk Block 9 highlighted that enhancer landscapes constrain τ_v by biasing transcriptional drift along particular eigendirections of the epigenetic state tensor, effectively tilting Γ_t so that some lineages experience locally high κ at early stages of dissemination while others encounter it only after prolonged dormancy. These differences imply that a universal scheduling policy will underperform relative to one that resolves lineage-restricted curvature fields, as the latter can anticipate when and where the ε-tube will tighten and preemptively deliver CRISPR constructs to exploit the compressed decision horizon. At the molecular level, this anticipatory control must contend with the kinetics of dCas9-KRAB or CRISPRa-mediated remodeling, which themselves alter the curvature of Γ_t by rewriting the epigenetic potential that defines the manifold’s shape, introducing a feedback loop in which intervention modifies the very geometry that governs its own efficacy. Consequently, the commitment window is not a static interval but a moving target whose position and width are co-determined by the cell’s intrinsic chromatin topology, microenvironmental cues, and the history of therapeutic perturbations, necessitating a closed-loop stochastic control scheme that updates κ estimates in real time and adjusts dosing schedules accordingly.

Integrating these principles, the curvature-guided timing framework posits that metastatic lethality can be attenuated by treating commitment windows as geometric objects rather than phenomenological periods, with κ serving as the primary control parameter that dictates both the urgency and the modality of intervention. This perspective refines earlier convex geometric models by embedding them within a stochastic control architecture capable of navigating the non-stationary landscape of disseminated tumor cells, wherein observability through live-cell epigenetic sensors and the fidelity of curvature estimation become rate-limiting steps. By aligning CRISPR delivery with the instantaneous geometry of Γ_t, the approach seeks to transform metastatic burden from a problem of cell number into one of controllable state transitions, exploiting the mechanistic link between curvature and commitment kinetics to push trajectories irreversibly toward eradication. The background thus sets the stage for a hypothesis-driven investigation of curvature-adaptive scheduling, grounded in the measurable prediction that higher local κ reduces τ_v in a lineage-specific manner and that real-time control policies leveraging this signal can significantly increase the eradication ratio relative to static protocols, while failure modes will emerge when curvature signals are obscured by sensor noise, lineage-specific enhancer buffering, or incomplete observability of the tubular neighborhood.

## 3. Mechanism

The integration of Adaptive Curvature-Weighted Stochastic Control (ACWSC) with optimal stopping theory constitutes the mechanistic backbone of this framework, translating geometric insights into a mathematically rigorous strategy for targeting metastatic commitment windows. At its core, the model leverages the state-dependent risk function $ r(x) = \exp(-\alpha\kappa(x)) $, where $ \kappa(x) $ denotes the instantaneous Gaussian curvature of the commitment manifold $ \Gamma_t $ at state $ x $, and $ \alpha $ is a tunable sensitivity parameter. This formulation embeds curvature as a direct modulator of therapeutic intervention timing, with higher curvature regions—indicative of geometrically constrained transcriptional-epigenetic states—corresponding to elevated risk thresholds. By framing the control problem within a Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) framework, the policy dynamically balances the trade-off between intervention efficacy and stochastic uncertainty, ensuring that CRISPR-Cas delivery is synchronized with the kinetic urgency encoded by $ \kappa(x) $. This approach extends the convex geometric control principles established in CancerHawk Block 9, where the eradication of disseminated tumor cells (DTCs) was shown to depend critically on lineage-specific curvature barriers, but introduces a real-time, adaptive layer through stochastic optimal control.  

The ACWSC model operationalizes curvature as a modulator of both intervention timing and risk aversion. In regions of high curvature, where $ \kappa(x) $ exceeds a critical threshold $ \kappa_c $, the exponential risk function $ r(x) $ decays sharply, reflecting the heightened vulnerability of DTCs to fate-determining transitions. Conversely, in low-curvature regions, where $ \kappa(x) < \kappa_c $, the risk function plateaus, signaling reduced therapeutic priority. This gradient-based policy ensures that CRISPR-Cas activation is concentrated in geometrically unstable regions of $ \Gamma_t $, where the probability of DTCs exiting the proliferative basin and entering drug-tolerant states is maximized. The Lyapunov function $ L(t,x) $, defined as $ L(t,x) = \int_t^\infty e^{-\lambda s} r(x(s)) ds $, governs stability by enforcing a convexity constraint on the control trajectory. When the local curvature $ \kappa_L $ of the control path exceeds $ \kappa_c $, the Lyapunov function decays, ensuring that the system is driven toward the target state (DTC eradication) while avoiding metastable regions of the commitment manifold. This stability criterion formalizes the intuition from CancerHawk Block 9 that curvature governs the convexity of commitment windows, but operationalizes it through a feedback loop that adjusts intervention intensity based on real-time curvature measurements.  

Real-time curvature estimation is enabled by Commitment-Potential Indicator (CPI) sensors, which report proximity to $ \Gamma_t $ via fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) between lineage-specific epigenetic markers and transcriptional activity reporters. These sensors generate a continuous, high-dimensional signal $ \mathbf{s}(t) $ that is filtered through a Kalman-Bucy estimator to produce a smoothed curvature estimate $ \hat{\kappa}(t) $. The Kalman-Bucy framework is particularly well-suited to this task, as it accounts for both measurement noise in the CPI signal and process noise arising from stochastic fluctuations in chromatin topology and microenvironmental pressures. By integrating $ \hat{\kappa}(t) $ into the ACWSC policy, the system achieves a closed-loop control mechanism that adapts to transient changes in the commitment landscape. This dynamic adjustment is critical, as the curvature of $ \Gamma_t $ evolves nonlinearly in response to both intrinsic epigenetic drift and extrinsic niche signals, such as cytokine gradients or mechanical stress.  

The ACWSC model’s efficacy hinges on its ability to distinguish between transient curvature fluctuations and persistent geometric features of $ \Gamma_t $. To achieve this, the policy incorporates a persistence threshold $ \tau_p $, derived from the autocorrelation of $ \hat{\kappa}(t) $ over a rolling window of $ \tau_p $ time units. Curvature estimates with autocorrelation values below a predefined $ \epsilon $-threshold are classified as noise and ignored, preventing spurious CRISPR activations in regions where the commitment manifold is topologically stable. This noise-filtering mechanism addresses a key limitation of prior work (CancerHawk Block 10), where static scheduling strategies failed to account for the dynamic interplay between curvature and transcriptional noise. By contrast, the ACWSC framework operationalizes curvature as a time-varying control parameter, enabling the system to prioritize interventions in regions where geometric instability is both persistent and biologically significant.  

The measurable endpoint of this approach—the eradication ratio—is defined as the proportion of barcoded DTC lineages eliminated relative to a static-schedule control, with a falsifiable hypothesis that curvature-guided scheduling will yield a ≥1.5-fold increase in eradication. This endpoint is directly tied to the model’s mechanistic assumptions: higher eradication ratios are expected when the policy successfully concentrates CRISPR-Cas delivery in high-$ \kappa $ regions, where DTCs are most susceptible to fate redirection. Conversely, the false-positive rate—the fraction of CRISPR activations occurring outside the $ \epsilon $-tubular neighborhood $ \mathcal{N}_\epsilon(\Gamma_t) $—serves as a secondary metric to quantify the precision of curvature estimation and policy execution. A failure mode arises if the eradication ratio does not improve significantly, indicating that either the CPI sensor’s curvature signal is obscured by lineage-specific chromatin heterogeneity or the Kalman-Bucy filter fails to adequately suppress noise in the commitment manifold’s geometry.  

This mechanism represents a paradigm shift in metastatic therapy, moving beyond static, population-averaged treatment paradigms to a cell-autonomous, geometry-driven intervention strategy. By formalizing curvature as a modulator of therapeutic risk and integrating real-time sensor data into a stochastic control framework, the ACWSC model provides a principled means of exploiting the geometric constraints of commitment windows. The Lyapunov stability criterion ensures that interventions are both temporally precise and robust to stochastic variability, while the Kalman-Bucy filtering mechanism enables adaptive responses to the nonlinear dynamics of $ \Gamma_t $. These elements collectively establish a testable, falsifiable framework for translating geometric insights into clinically actionable strategies, with the potential to disrupt the metastatic cascade at its most fundamental level.
