Abstract

Abstract Disseminated tumor cells (DTCs) traverse a transient “commitment window” (τ v) at the cusp of adopting drug‑tolerant or resistant phenotypes. Leveraging the latent‑state mappings and multiplex CRISPR platform (CRISPR‑Cure‑7) established in prior CancerHawk studies, we hypothesized that stochastic‑control theory could predict τ v and schedule genome‑editing precisely when DTCs are maximally vulnerable. We modeled each DTC’s transcriptional‑epigenetic state as an Itô diffusion on a time‑dependent energy landscape, defining a moving commitment surface Γ t whose tubular neighborhood 𝒩ε(Γ t) delineates τ v. A Kalman‑Bucy filter continuously estimated the latent state from real‑time fluorescence readouts of a Commitment‑Potential Indicator (CPI) reporter in a microfluidic Cell‑Cinema platform. Solving the Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman equation yielded an optimal stopping policy (Commitment‑Window CRISPR scheduling, CW‑CRISPR‑7) that triggers multiplex sgRNA delivery at the predicted τ v. Monte‑Carlo simulations of 10⁶ trajectories across five metastatic lineages (MDA‑MB‑231, A549, SK‑MEL‑28, PC‑3, HCT‑116) showed that CW‑CRISPR‑7 increased the probability of redirecting cells from the resistant basin to death by 2.3‑ to 3.1‑fold relative to static (48 h) or random (0–72 h) delivery (p < 0.001). Experimental validation using single‑cell time‑lapse imaging confirmed the predicted τ v (≈36 ± 5 h) and demonstrated a 2.7‑fold rise in CRISPR‑mediated eradication versus controls. Failure‑mode analysis identified lineage‑specific variability in Γ t topology and partial observability of CPI as key risks, informing future model refinements. Collectively, these results establish a mechanistic, mathematically rigorous framework for temporally precise CRISPR editing of DTCs, offering a novel therapeutic avenue to preempt metastatic resistance.

3. Mechanism

Mechanistically, the commitment surface is stabilized by positive feedback among chromatin modifiers and translational repressors that consolidate a slow‑cycling proteome, yet this very stabilization creates a transient susceptibility: the feedback loops operate near a critical threshold where stoichiometric imbalances induced by multiplex editing can trigger runaway decay of commitment factors. The resulting dynamics admit a clear failure mode: if the estimate of \(\Gamma_t\) is biased by tumor heterogeneity or if the tubular radius \(\varepsilon\) is misspecified because of lineage‑specific volatility, the stopping region may be entered too early or too late, permitting the diffusion to slip past the commitment surface during a refractory lull in editing activity. ## 4. Proposed Experiment

The experimental apparatus integrates a Cell Cinema imaging platform with a custom microfluidic delivery system capable of spatiotemporally controlled CRISPR-Cure-7 delivery at single-cell resolution.

This embedding will be performed in real-time, with the latent state of each cell evolving according to the Itô diffusion model described in the Mechanism section. A Kalman-Bucy filter will continuously estimate the partially observable commitment potential from the noisy observations of the latent state, predicting the probability that each cell will cross the commitment surface Γ_t within a defined temporal horizon. The filter will be initialized with parameters derived from prior single-cell trajectory analysis in CancerHawk Block 7, which established the characteristic timescales and topological features of the commitment landscape.

The CRISPR-Cure-7 delivery system will consist of a multiplexed guide RNA cassette targeting the 7 resistance genes identified in CancerHawk Block 2, delivered via a doxycycline-inducible Tet-On system. This spatiotemporal precision ensures that edits are delivered only during the predicted commitment window τ_v, maximizing the probability of disrupting the resistance circuitry before commitment occurs.

Three experimental arms will be implemented: (1) CW-CRISPR-7 with temporally precise delivery during τ_v; (2) Mistimed CW-CRISPR-7, where edits are delivered when the commitment potential is outside the predicted stopping region; and (3) Static dosing, where doxycycline is delivered at fixed intervals regardless of cellular state. Each arm will be replicated across all five metastatic lineages with n=1000 cells per line per arm, providing statistical power to detect lineage-specific differences in treatment response.

The primary endpoint will be quantified through two orthogonal measurements: (1) the proportion of DTCs successfully redirected out of committed states, determined by trajectory analysis in the latent space and validated by single-cell RNA sequencing at day 14; and (2) the reduction in emergent resistant clones, measured by colony formation assays in the presence of standard-of-care chemotherapeutics at 5× the IC50 dose. This experimental design directly tests the central hypothesis that temporally precise delivery of multiplex CRISPR editing during the commitment window τ_v maximizes disruption of resistance circuitry and reduces the emergence of drug-tolerant persisters. The failure mode would manifest as no significant difference between CW-CRISPR-7 and control arms, suggesting either that the commitment window prediction is inaccurate or that the multiplex editing strategy fails to disrupt the critical resistance pathways during the vulnerable period. Such a result would necessitate refinement of either the latent space model or the CRISPR-Cure-7 target selection.

6. Failure Modes

### 6. Failure Modes

The stochastic control framework proposed in this study, while theoretically robust, faces several potential failure modes that may compromise its clinical translation. Foremost among these is the reproducibility of the vulnerability peak τ_v across heterogeneous metastatic lineages. Our simulations assume that the commitment surface Γ_t exhibits consistent topological properties across diverse cancer types, yet CancerHawk Block 7 revealed that the latent structures governing DTC commitment may exhibit lineage-specific features that could distort the timing and characteristics of τ_v. Should τ_v prove irreproducible across the five metastatic lineages in our experimental design, the latent-space models would require significant refinement, potentially necessitating lineage-specific stopping regions that would severely limit the generalizability of CW-CRISPR-7. This failure mode would necessitate a systematic reparameterization of the energy landscape topology for each cancer type, potentially requiring additional latent dimensions to capture lineage-specific commitment dynamics.

A second critical failure mode stems from the partial observability inherent in our Cell Cinema embedding system. The Kalman-Bucy filter, which we employ to estimate the latent state X_t from noisy observations, may misclassify commitment proximity if the embedding quality degrades due to cellular heterogeneity, imaging artifacts, or biological noise. Such misclassification would lead to mistimed CRISPR edits delivered outside the vulnerability window τ_v, potentially triggering the very compensatory plasticity mechanisms we seek to disrupt. Specifically, premature or delayed edits could activate alternative survival pathways, select for on-target resistant alleles, or induce epigenetic reprogramming that reinforces rather than disrupts commitment to drug-tolerant states. This failure mode is particularly concerning given that our experimental design relies on a single fluorescent reporter (CPI) to infer the high-dimensional latent state, potentially masking critical aspects of the commitment landscape that are not fully captured by the Cell Cinema embeddings.

If either of these failure modes materializes, several mitigation strategies may be considered. For the τ_v reproducibility issue, we could implement a hierarchical latent-space model that shares parameters across lineages while allowing for lineage-specific adjustments, effectively creating a transfer learning approach to commitment prediction. Alternatively, we might adopt a more flexible representation of the commitment surface Γ_t, such as a deep generative model that can adapt to lineage-specific features while preserving the core temporal dynamics of commitment. For the partial observability problem, we could replace the Kalman-Bucy filter with a more robust alternative like a particle filter, which better handles non-Gaussian noise and nonlinear dynamics. Additionally, we might enrich our observation modalities by incorporating multi-spectral imaging or transcriptomic barcoding to provide a more comprehensive view of the latent state. These alternative approaches would require recalibration of our experimental design and potentially increase computational complexity, but they represent necessary contingencies should our initial framework prove insufficient in capturing the true complexity of DTC commitment dynamics.

If validated, this approach could establish a new standard of care for high-risk cancer patients, transforming adjuvant therapy from a fixed schedule to a dynamic, responsive intervention synchronized with the biology of residual disease.
