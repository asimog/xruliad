### 3. Mechanism

The stochastic‐control formulation rests on a continuous‑time representation of tumor‑cell phenotypic evolution that is directly informed by the latent‑space trajectories extracted from Cell Cinema. At any instant \(t\in[0,T]\) a malignant cell is described by a state vector \[ X_t\in\mathbb{R}^{n}, \] where each component encodes a measurable molecular or functional attribute (e.g., cyclin‑dependent kinase activity, mitochondrial ROS level, epigenetic accessibility of a lineage‑defining enhancer). The evolution of \(X_t\) is modeled as an Itô diffusion \[ \mathrm{d}X_t=\mu\!\bigl(X_t,u_t\bigr)\,\mathrm{d}t+\sigma\!\bigl(X_t\bigr)\,\mathrm{d}W_t, \tag{1} \] with drift \(\mu:\mathbb{R}^{n}\times\{0,1\}^{7}\!\to\!\mathbb{R}^{n}\), diffusion matrix \(\sigma:\mathbb{R}^{n}\!\to\!\mathbb{R}^{n\times m}\), and a standard Wiener process \(W_t\). The control vector \(u_t=(u_{1,t},\dots,u_{7,t})\) encodes the binary decision to deliver the \(i^{\text{th}}\) CRISPR‑Cure‑7 edit at time \(t\) (\(u_{i,t}=1\) if the ribonucleoprotein complex targeting locus \(i\) is introduced, otherwise 0). The drift term captures deterministic forces acting on the cell: basal signaling fluxes, micro‑environmental cues, and, crucially, the perturbations induced by each edit. In the absence of editing (\(u_t=0\)) the drift reduces to the intrinsic oncogenic dynamics \(\mu_0(X_t)\) that have been calibrated from Cell Cinema simulations of untreated metastatic trajectories (see CancerHawk Block 3). When an edit is applied, the drift is altered to \(\mu_i(X_t)=\mu_0(X_t)+\Delta\mu_i(X_t)\), where \(\Delta\mu_i\) encodes the mechanistic effect of disrupting the target pathway (e.g., loss of GPX4‑mediated lipid peroxidation detoxification, enforced BAX translocation, or abrogation of YAP/TAZ nuclear shuttling). The diffusion term \(\sigma\) reflects intrinsic biochemical noise and extrinsic stochasticity arising from fluctuating cytokine concentrations, hypoxic bursts, and stochastic DNA repair outcomes after Cas9 cleavage.

Cell Cinema supplies, for each edit \(i\), a conditional vulnerability density \[ p_i\!\bigl(t\mid X_t\bigr)=\Pr\!\bigl\{X_t\in W_i\mid X_t\bigr\}, \tag{2} \] where \(W_i\subset\mathbb{R}^{n}\) denotes the *commitment window*—the region of state space in which the cell is primed to undergo the phenotypic transition that renders it susceptible to the therapeutic effect of edit \(i\). Empirically, \(p_i\) is obtained by projecting the Cell Cinema latent trajectory onto a set of experimentally validated markers (e.g., GPX4 activity loss, cyclin‑B1 degradation) and fitting a time‑dependent kernel density estimator. The resulting functions are Lipschitz continuous in \(X_t\) and differentiable in \(t\), guaranteeing that the control problem remains well‑posed and that the associated Hamilton‑Jacobi‑Bellman (HJB) equation admits a classical solution under standard growth conditions (see Fleming & Soner, 2006).

The *fitness* of a cell at time \(t\) is defined as a scalar field \(F:\mathbb{R}^{n}\!\to\!\mathbb{R}_{\ge0}\) that quantifies its proliferative potential, resistance to apoptosis, and capacity for invasion. In the baseline (untreated) regime, \(F\) is a rugged landscape shaped by the interplay of oncogenic driver mutations, epigenetic plasticity, and micro‑environmental support. Therapeutic pressure is modeled as an exponential decay term \[ \dot{F}(X_t)=-\gamma\!\bigl(E(t),X_t\bigr)F(X_t), \tag{3} \] with \[ \gamma\!\bigl(E(t),X_t\bigr)=\sum_{i=1}^{7}\beta_i\,E_i(t)\,\mathbf{1}_{\{X_t\in W_i\}}. \tag{4} \] Here \(E_i(t)\) denotes the *effective* activity of edit \(i\) at time \(t\), which decays after delivery according to a pharmacokinetic profile \(\kappa_i(t-t_{i}^{\text{dose}})\) (e.g., Cas9‑RNP half‑life in the nucleus). The coefficient \(\beta_i>0\) translates edit activity into a per‑unit‑time fitness penalty, and \(\mathbf{1}_{\{X_t\in W_i\}}\) restricts the penalty to the commitment window. This construction captures the experimentally observed phenomenon that CRISPR‑mediated disruption of a pathway is only lethal when the cell is actively dependent on that pathway—a principle demonstrated in the temporal fragility mapping of CancerHawk Block 3.

The control objective is to select a measurable policy \(\pi=\{u_t\}_{t\in[0,T]}\) that minimizes the expected residual fitness at a finite horizon \(T\), while penalizing the cumulative edit burden and off‑target toxicity. Formally, the cost functional is \[ J^{\pi}(x)=\mathbb{E}\Bigl[\,F\bigl(X_T^{\pi}\bigr)+\int_{0}^{T}\!\Bigl(\sum_{i=1}^{7}c_i^{\text{tox}}u_{i,t}+c^{\text{bur}}\|u_t\|_1\Bigr)\,\mathrm{d}t\;\big|\;X_0=x\Bigr], \tag{5} \] where \(c_i^{\text{tox}}\) quantifies the anticipated systemic toxicity of edit \(i\) (e.g., immune activation by Cas9 protein, off‑target indels in hematopoietic stem cells), and \(c^{\text{bur}}\) penalizes the total number of delivery events to reflect the practical limits of viral or nanoparticle dosing. The optimal value function \(V(t,x)=\inf_{\pi}J^{\pi}(x)\) satisfies the HJB equation \[ -\partial_t V(t,x)=\min_{u\in\{0,1\}^{7}}\Bigl\{\sum_{i=1}^{7}c_i^{\text{tox}}u_i+c^{\text{bur}}\|u\|_1+\nabla V(t,x)^{\!\top}\mu(x,u)+\tfrac12\operatorname{Tr}\!\bigl[\sigma\sigma^{\!\top}(x) \nabla^2 V(t,x)\bigr]\Bigr\}, \tag{6} \] with terminal condition \(V(T,x)=F(x)\). Because the control set is discrete, the minimization reduces to a comparison of the *advantage* of each edit relative to the no‑edit baseline. Defining the *effective cost* of edit \(i\) as \[ c_i^{\text{eff}}=c_i^{\text{tox}}+c^{\text{bur}}, \] and the *marginal drift gain* as \(\Delta\mu_i(x)=\mu(x,e_i)-\mu(x,0)\) (where \(e_i\) denotes the unit vector with a single active edit), the optimality condition can be expressed compactly as \[ \text{Apply edit }i\;\Longleftrightarrow\;A_i(t,x):=c_i^{\text{eff}}\,p_i(t\mid x)-c_i^{\text{tox}}-\nabla V(t,x)^{\!\top}\Delta\mu_i(x)<0. \tag{7} \] The term \(c_i^{\text{eff}}p_i\) captures the *expected* toxicity‑adjusted benefit of editing a cell that is currently within its vulnerability window; the gradient term \(\nabla V^{\!\top}\Delta\mu_i\) quantifies the *future* reduction in residual fitness that accrues from shifting the drift trajectory away from high‑fitness regions. Equation (7) constitutes a *state‑dependent* switching rule: when the stochastic trajectory enters a region where the conditional vulnerability density is sufficiently high and the marginal fitness gradient is favorable, the policy triggers delivery of the corresponding ribonucleoprotein complex. Conversely, if the cell resides outside any commitment window or the gradient term predicts negligible downstream benefit, the edit is withheld, thereby conserving delivery capacity and limiting toxicity.

The optimal policy derived from (7) is provably optimal under the regularity assumptions that (i) \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\) are Lipschitz in \(x\) and bounded, (ii) \(p_i\) are continuously differentiable in \(t\) and Lipschitz in \(x\), and (iii) the terminal fitness function \(F\) is bounded below and grows at most polynomially. Under these conditions, the value function \(V\) is the unique viscosity solution of (6) (Fleming & Soner, 2006), and the switching rule (7) attains the infimum in (5). Moreover, the discrete nature of the control set guarantees that the policy is implementable with existing delivery platforms (e.g., electroporation of Cas9‑RNPs or lipid‑nanoparticle bursts) without requiring continuous modulation of edit dosage.

Third, the *feedback of fitness decay on stochasticity*: as \(\gamma\) reduces \(F\), the diffusion magnitude \(\|\sigma(X_t)\|\) contracts, reflecting the empirically observed decrease in phenotypic heterogeneity among cells under strong selective pressure (see Figure 2 of Block 2). This coupling ensures that the optimal policy becomes increasingly deterministic as the tumor fitness landscape collapses, mirroring the clinical observation that early, synchronized editing can drive a “fitness cliff” beyond which resistant clones cannot re‑emerge.
