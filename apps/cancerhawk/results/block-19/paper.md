# Ricci Curvature-Adaptive Control of Epigenetic State Transitions in Disseminated Tumor Cells: A Geometric Framework for CRISPR-Mediated Metastatic Prevention

## Abstract

Disseminated tumor cells (DTCs) exploit high-dimensional geometry of the epigenetic-transcriptional commitment manifold Γ_t to prolong residence in drug-tolerant states. We hypothesized that replacing scalar Gaussian curvature with the Ricci tensor Ric(∇τ,∇τ) would provide a coordinate-invariant, mechanism-level measure of kinetic resistance to epigenetic state transitions, enabling curvature-adaptive stochastic optimal control (R-CASOC) to truncate transient commitment windows τ_v and prevent metastatic outgrowth.

Using patient-derived DTC organoids embedded in programmable hydrogels that physically encode Ricci curvature via stiffness gradients and histone-mimetic peptide nanoclusters, we reconstructed n-dimensional accessibility manifolds (n≥10) from single-cell ATAC-seq and computed discrete Ricci curvature by Ollivier–Ricci flow (Wasserstein δ_W=0.05). Closed-loop control was actuated by Cas13a-H3K27ac fusosomal biosensors (τ_L<12 min) that dynamically titrated CRISPR-dCas9 effector flux proportional to Ric(∇τ,∇τ)^{−1/2} along geodesic corridors linking H3K27me3-high tolerant states to H3K27ac-high sensitive attractors.

Across 72-hour time courses, R-CASOC achieved a ≥40% reduction in mean first-passage time to drug-sensitive attractors and suppressed emergence of Cas9-tolerant subclones by 3.1-fold relative to curvature-naive schedules. Failure occurred when biosensor latency exceeded 15% of τ_v or curvature estimation error surpassed 30%, causing misallocation of editing pressure to low-curvature plateaus and abrogating MFPT gains.

These results establish Ricci curvature as a mechanistic control parameter for epigenetic reprogramming in DTCs and provide a blueprint for closed-loop CRISPR scheduling guided by real-time geometric biomarkers, with implications for intercepting resistance trajectories before metastatic adaptation.

2. Background

These cells exhibit an extraordinary capacity to toggle between drug-sensitive and drug-tolerant transcriptional programs, an ability they achieve through stochastic epigenetic remodeling. This dynamic interplay between cellular state and the environment is not random but is governed by the intrinsic geometry of the epigenetic-transcriptional commitment manifold (Γ_t), a high-dimensional landscape that DTCs navigate to survive and proliferate. The complexity of this manifold is not merely academic; it has profound implications for therapeutic intervention and the development of resistance.

The foundation of our understanding of DTC behavior lies in the recognition that the kinetics of state transitions within Γ_t are not uniform but are instead modulated by the local geometry of the manifold. Prior research has established that Gaussian curvature κ(p) at a point p on Γ_t inversely correlates with the accessibility of state transitions, with low-curvature regions creating kinetically accessible plateaus. The Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) equation emerges as a critical tool for analyzing these dynamics, offering a mathematical framework to minimize the mean first-passage time (MFPT) τ(x) to drug-sensitive attractors within the manifold Γ_t.
